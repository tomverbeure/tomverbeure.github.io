# Introduction

Even since getting my 
[Rohde & Schwarz AMIQ vector signal analyzer](/2025/04/26/RS-AMIQ-Teardown-Analog-Deep-Dive.html) 
to work, I've been on the lookout for a suitable RF companion to upconvert the AMIQ I/Q baseband 
output signal to whichever RF carrier frequency is needed. The obvious choice is an
[R&S SMIQ](https://zw-ix.nl/blog/2021/02/09/494/): 
it can even control the AMIQ directly, no external PC necessary, but they tend to be relatively expensive, 
think $500 and up. I eventually settled for an R&S SFQ TV Test Transmitter that I found for $200 at a 
liquidator in Sacramento. Its designed to create digital TV signals on the unit itself, but this functionality 
can be bypassed and the RF section can be fed with external I/Q inputs. The SFQ isn't perfect though: its biggest
limitation is an analog bandwidth of only 20 MHz, too low to cover the spectrum of 
[40 2 MHz Bluetooth LE channels](/2026/03/05/Polyphase-Channelizer-with-Offset.html#a-bluetooth-le-trace-as-example).

Still, I was happy enough, I even passed on a $200 SMIQ02 at the 
[Silicon Valley Electronics Flea Market](https://www.electronicsfleamarket.com):
the price was right, but a maximum RF frequency of 2 GHz is just too limiting.

Fast forward to last weekend for yet another flea market, and I ran into this beauty:

![MG3700A in the trunk of my car](/assets/mg3700a/mg3700a_in_the_car.jpg)

An Artisu MG3700A vector signal generator for the absurd price of $75. Other than some easy
to remove stickers, it looked clean, the seller assured me that it worked fine[^seller], it even
included a USB stick with all the manuals, firmware updates, and Windows control software.

[^seller]: Hobbyist sellers at this flea market tend to be pretty honest about the functional
           state of what they sell.

I have no idea why it was priced so low, I definitely didn't ask, but it validated my whole 
getting-up-at-6:15am routine to get access to the good stuff. As an extra bonus, I was also
able to pick up my first 20 GHz microwave frequency counter, an HP 5350B, for a very reasonable $100.
They're a match made in heaven:

![MG3700A and 5350B transmitting and measuring a 3 GHz signal](/assets/mg3700a/siggen_and_counter.jpg)

So, yes, they both worked great!

# The Anritsu MG3700A

The MG3700A was introduced in 2004, which is pretty recent by hobbyist test equipment standards. According
to the date on the hard drive, mine was produced sometime around 2008 and the calibration certificate(!)
on the USB stick shows that it was last calibrated in 2016, which is practically yesterday.
